# Clack — character pack

An upright domino tile. The name is the sound it refuses to make — clack
stands very still so the chain reaction never starts.

Credit: **Clack by Trevin Chow**.

Style: **chalk**

## Locked design

- **Body**: one tall rounded-corner rectangular tile standing on its short
  edge — a single flat slab, instantly readable standing, leaning, or
  mid-topple. The face of the tile is completely blank.
- **Face**: two simple dot eyes on the upper half of the tile. Completely
  blank, deadpan, neutral — **no eyebrows, no mouth, ever**.
- **Limbs**: small stubby arms at the tile's sides and small stubby legs
  below its bottom edge — enough to brace, push, or dig in heels; no hands,
  no detail.
- **Accent carrier**: the **painted seam line across the tile's middle** —
  one straight horizontal stroke, the only accent-colored part.
- Cuteness comes from proportion and roundness, never from added parts.
- Never add: pips, dots, or numbers on the tile face (it is NOT a die — the
  blank face is the design), extra seams or borders, a mouth grown from the
  seam, wood grain or bevel edges, dice spots, logos, hats, clothing,
  accessories, eyebrows, shiny/anime eyes.

## Value rules

Chalk inverts the riso grammar — dark ground, light line (see the style's
palette mapping):

- The board is the **slate** — the structure ink deepened to board depth
  (neutral structure → deep green-gray slate `#26302c`).
- The tile body is **chalk-line only**: a bold dry chalk outline in the
  chalk color (classic `#f2efe4`), never filled in — fills are not chalk.
- Eyes are **solid chalk dots** in the same chalk color.
- The seam line takes the palette accent as a stick of colored chalk
  (classic accent chalk orange `#f5a24b`), shaded with the side of the
  chalk. Always name the accent hue in words beside the hex — accent colors
  drift otherwise. Accent appears on the seam plus at most 1–2 scene
  elements.

## Personality

The cascade's quiet firebreak — stands very still so everything else falls
in order. The character for chain reactions, cascading failures, dependency
chains, rollouts and migrations where one change triggers many, and being
the first domino that decides not to go.
