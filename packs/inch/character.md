# Inch — character pack

A plump chalk inchworm, arched mid-step. The name is the unit — the thing
that covers a journey one verified body-length at a time.

Credit: **Inch by Trevin Chow**.

Style: **chalk**
Cutout chroma: **magenta**
Aliases: inchworm, caterpillar

## Locked design

- **Body**: one smooth plump tube bent into a low arch, like a lowercase
  n — uniform thickness end to end, no taper, no segments. The arch height
  is fixed: always drawn mid-step at the same low arch, so the silhouette
  never changes between renders.
- **Face**: two simple solid chalk-dot eyes on the front end of the tube.
  Completely blank, deadpan, neutral — **no eyebrows, no mouth, ever**.
- **Feet**: exactly two stubby gripper pads, one at each end of the arch,
  planted flat on the ground — soft round stubs, no toes, no extra legs
  anywhere along the body.
- **Accent carrier**: the **rear gripper pad** — the back foot it anchors
  with before every new step — the only accent-colored part. The front end
  carries the eyes; the rear end carries the accent, so the two ends are
  never ambiguous.
- Cuteness comes from proportion and roundness, never from added parts.
- Never add: body segments or ring lines, extra legs or prolegs, antennae,
  fuzz/hairs/bristles, stripes or patterns, a smile or mouth, eyebrows,
  leaves or apples, teeth, accessories, accent on the front end or both
  ends.

## Value rules

Chalk inverts the usual grammar — dark ground, light line (see the style's
palette mapping):

- The body is a **chalk-line drawing only** — bold dry chalk strokes in the
  chalk color (paper lifted to chalk white, e.g. `#f2efe4`) on the deep
  slate board; no fills, ever.
- Eyes are **solid chalk dots** in the same chalk white.
- The rear gripper pad is **shaded in with the side of the accent chalk**
  (classic default: warm chalk orange `#f5a24b`). Always name the accent
  hue in words beside the hex — accent chalk drifts otherwise. The front
  pad stays plain chalk white in every palette.

## Personality

Unhurried and undeterred — one small step, then the next, then the next.
Good for steady progress, patience, and incremental work and migrations.
