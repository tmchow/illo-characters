# Scrub — character pack

A plump beveled clay eraser. The name is the job — the thing that scrubs
out whatever no longer needs to exist.

Credit: **Scrub by Trevin Chow**.

Style: **clay**

## Locked design

- **Body**: one low chunky parallelogram lying horizontal — the classic
  slant-ended eraser wedge, longer than tall, soft rounded corners,
  hand-modeled in matte clay.
- **Face**: two simple clay-bead dot eyes on the long face. Completely
  blank, deadpan, neutral — **no eyebrows, no mouth, ever**.
- **Limbs**: small stubby arms at the sides and short stubby legs beneath
  the block — soft nubs, no hands, no fingers spelled out.
- **Accent carrier**: the darker scuffed bevel at one slanted end — its
  working edge — the only accent-colored part.
- Cuteness comes from proportion and roundness, never from added parts.
- Never add: a paper sleeve or wrapper, stamped brand lettering, an attached
  pencil or brush, segments or hard facets, sculpted crumb texture on the
  body, glossy sheen, teeth, eyebrows, mouth, hats, clothing, accessories.
  The wedge stays horizontal — never stood upright like a battery block.

> leaning its whole body weight into the work. The mascot itself is a
> hand-modeled matte clay figure exactly like every other object in the set
> — never a flat drawing. {value rule: body in light set-toned clay so it
> reads as soft white rubber, eyes as main-clay beads, the working-edge
> bevel in matte accent clay}

## Value rules

Clay maps value to material color (see the style's palette mapping):

- The body is always **set-toned clay** — the mapped paper color, kept a
  touch lighter than the backdrop so the block reads as soft white rubber
  and separates by light, in every palette.
- Eyes are beads in the **main clay** (the structure ink lifted to a clay
  material color, keeping its temperature).
- The working-edge bevel takes the palette accent as matte accent clay.
  Always name the accent hue in words beside the hex — clay renders drift
  accent color toward toy defaults.

## Personality

The unsentimental remover — no ceremony for anything that no longer needs
to exist. The character for cleanup and removal: refactoring, deleting dead
code, paying down tech debt, tidying after a launch, unsubscribing,
decluttering — any story about making something smaller and cleaner.
